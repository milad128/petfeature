# PM Learning Roadmap — Beginner to CPO

> A structured, opinionated learning path for product managers at every career stage.
> Built on Marty Cagan's *Empowered* competency framework.
> Last validated: July 2026.

---

## How to read this document

Each learning block contains:
- **Goal** — what you'll be able to do after
- **Key concepts** — what to focus on while studying
- **Time** — estimated study time for this block
- **Resources** — with type, reading time, difficulty, and Persian translation status
- **Practice** — one concrete exercise to apply what you learned
- **Why this, not that** — where relevant, why this resource over alternatives

**Difficulty legend:** ⭐ Easy · ⭐⭐ Medium · ⭐⭐⭐ Hard
**Persian:** 🇮🇷 = Persian translation available · 🔴 = No Persian translation known

## Level summary

| # | Level | Specification | Blocks | Study time | Time in role |
|---|-------|--------------|--------|-----------|--------------|
| 1 | **Beginner (APM)** | Learns PM fundamentals, contributes to execution under guidance, develops core analytical, technical, and communication skills. | 9 | 4–6 months | 12–24 months |
| 2 | **Product Manager (PM)** | Independently owns a product or feature, discovers customer problems, prioritizes solutions, and delivers measurable business outcomes. | 8 | 3–4 months | 24–36 months |
| 3 | **Senior Product Manager** | Solves ambiguous product problems, leads strategic initiatives, mentors other PMs, and influences direction across multiple teams. | 8 | 3–4 months | 24–36 months |
| 4 | **Product Lead** | Leads a small group of PMs, sets direction for a significant product area, bridges IC excellence and people leadership. | 8 | 3–4 months | 12–24 months |
| 5 | **Director of Product** | Builds and leads product teams, defines strategy and operating processes, aligns investment with business objectives. | 7 | 3–4 months | 36–60 months |
| 6 | **CPO** | Owns the company's product vision and portfolio, shapes long-term strategy, and aligns executive leadership around market opportunities. | 6 | 2–3 months | Open-ended |

> **Time in role** = how long to actually work at that level before leveling up. Study time alone is not enough — you need reps.

**Full path: 46 blocks · ~18–25 months of structured study · 10–17 years of career time**

```
Beginner (APM) → PM → Senior PM → Product Lead → Director → CPO
```

## Level 1 — Beginner (APM)

**Time in role before moving up:** 12–24 months of actual PM work

### Minimum viable path (if you only do 3 blocks)
1. Block 4 — User Research Basics *(everything else depends on this)*
2. Block 1 — What is PM *(you can't learn the craft without knowing what it is)*
3. Block 5 — Writing Specs *(your core daily output)*

### Core outcomes (Cagan / *Empowered*)

| # | Outcome |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Customer Knowledge** — can talk to users, synthesize insights, and separate problems from solutions |
| 2 | **Technical Literacy** — understands how the product is built well enough to have credible conversations with engineers |
| 3 | **Data Literacy** — reads dashboards, defines basic success metrics, interprets results |
| 4 | **Delivery Contribution** — writes clear specs, participates in sprints, unblocks the team |
| 5 | **Communication** — documents decisions, updates stakeholders, runs structured meetings |

### Block 1 · What is PM / Role & Mindset

> **Goal:** Understand what a product manager actually does — and whether this is the right career for you.
> **Key concepts:** PM vs project manager vs owner, the product trio, discovery vs delivery, what "outcome" means.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Inspired* — Marty Cagan (ch. 1–5) | Book | 3 hrs (partial) | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Decode and Conquer* — Lewis Lin | Book | 4 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| *The Product Book* — Product School | Book | 5 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| "What is product management?" — Lenny Rachitsky | Article | 20 min | ⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *Inspired* and not a course first?** Cagan gives you the mental model; courses give you tasks. Build the model first, then the tasks make sense.

**Practice:** Interview one PM (in person or remotely) for 30 minutes. Ask: what does a typical week look like, what's the hardest part of your job, and what do you wish you'd known earlier. Write a 1-page summary.

### Block 2 · Agile & How Products Are Built

> **Goal:** Contribute confidently inside a product team from your first week on the job.
> **Key concepts:** Sprints, backlogs, user stories, ceremonies (standup, retro, planning), Lean thinking, Shape Up as an alternative.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Scrum* — Jeff Sutherland | Book | 4 hrs | ⭐ | 🇮🇷 |
| *The Lean Startup* — Eric Ries | Book | 7 hrs | ⭐ | 🇮🇷 |
| Atlassian Agile Coach | Online Guide | 2 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Shape Up* — Ryan Singer | Book | 5 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *Scrum* before *Shape Up*?** Scrum is what most teams run; Shape Up is the alternative you'll encounter later. Know the default first.

**Practice:** Map your current team's (or any product team's) delivery process: what happens between "idea" and "shipped"? Draw it as a flowchart. Identify where things get stuck.

### Block 3 · Technical Literacy

> **Goal:** Have credible conversations with engineers without needing to code.
> **Key concepts:** How the web works (client/server, APIs, databases), system architecture basics, trade-offs engineers navigate, reading technical docs.
> **Time:** 2–4 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *The Pragmatic Programmer* — Hunt & Thomas (ch. 1–5) | Book | 3 hrs (partial) | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *How the Internet Works* — Preston Gralla | Book | 4 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| Khan Academy: Computer Science | Online Course | 5 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| Codecademy: Intro to Programming | Online Course | 6 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why not a full programming course?** You're not becoming an engineer. Target: understand what's hard, what's easy, and what's possible. Stop there.

**Practice:** Pick any feature in your product. Ask an engineer to explain how it works technically. Then explain it back to a non-technical person. If you can do both, you've got enough.

### Block 4 · User Research Basics

> **Goal:** Talk to users and extract real insight — not just confirmation of what you already believe.
> **Key concepts:** The Mom Test principle, interview structure, asking problem-not-solution questions, synthesizing patterns from qualitative data.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *The Mom Test* — Rob Fitzpatrick | Book | 3 hrs | ⭐ | 🇮🇷 |
| *Just Enough Research* — Erika Hall | Book | 5 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Interviewing Users* — Steve Portigal | Book | 6 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| Nielsen Norman Group articles | Articles | 1–2 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *The Mom Test* first?** It's the fastest path from zero to not-wasting-your-interviews. Read it in one sitting before your first user call.

**Practice:** Conduct 5 user interviews using only The Mom Test question style (no solution-pitching, no leading questions). Write up the top 3 patterns you heard across all 5 people.

### Block 5 · Writing — Specs & PRDs

> **Goal:** Write clearly enough that engineers and designers can build the right thing without you in the room.
> **Key concepts:** Problem statement, user story format, acceptance criteria, edge cases, communicating tradeoffs in writing.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Writing for Busy Readers* — Todd Rogers & Jessica Levy | Book | 4 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| PRD templates — Lenny Rachitsky | Template / Article | 30 min | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| *On Writing Well* — William Zinsser | Book | 6 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| GitLab Product Handbook — spec writing | Online Guide | 1 hr | ⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *Writing for Busy Readers* over a PM-specific writing guide?** PM writing fails because people don't read it. This book solves the reading problem, not the writing problem.

**Practice:** Write a full PRD for a feature you use every day (e.g. Gmail's snooze, Spotify's playlist shuffle). Include: problem, user story, acceptance criteria, edge cases, and what you're explicitly *not* building.

### Block 6 · Basic Metrics & Data

> **Goal:** Know if your feature worked after you ship it.
> **Key concepts:** KPIs vs vanity metrics, funnels, conversion rates, retention, reading dashboards, avoiding metric misreads.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Lean Analytics* — Croll & Yoskovitz | Book | 8 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

| Reforge: Metrics articles | Articles | 2 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

---

### Ready for PM when:
- Shipped at least one feature end-to-end with minimal hand-holding
- Can explain *why* a decision was made, not just *what* was built
- Has conducted at least 10 user interviews and synthesized findings into clear insights
- Reads metrics independently and can tell the difference between a good result and a lucky one
- Writes specs engineers can build from without follow-up clarification

## Level 2 — Product Manager (PM)

**Time in role before moving up:** 24–36 months of owning a product area independently

### Minimum viable path (if you only do 3 blocks)
1. Block 1 — Continuous Discovery *(the core PM skill that separates good from great)*
2. Block 3 — Prioritization *(you can't do everything; this is how you decide)*
3. Block 4 — Outcomes Over Output *(the mindset shift that makes everything else work)*

### Core outcomes (Cagan / *Empowered*)

| # | Outcome |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Continuous Discovery** — runs ongoing product discovery to find solutions that are *valuable, usable, feasible, and viable* |
| 2 | **Product Strategy** — translates company objectives into clear priorities for their area; pushes back on weak ideas |
| 3 | **Outcome Ownership** — defines and tracks success metrics; accountable for business outcomes, not just shipping |
| 4 | **Cross-functional Leadership** — leads a product trio (PM, designer, tech lead) without formal authority |
| 5 | **Stakeholder Management** — manages expectations, earns trust, and communicates tradeoffs to leadership |

---

### Block 1 · Continuous Discovery

> **Goal:** Make user research a weekly habit, not a one-time project phase.
> **Key concepts:** Opportunity solution tree, assumption testing, continuous interviewing cadence, prototype-before-build mindset.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks to learn; lifetime to practice

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Continuous Discovery Habits* — Teresa Torres | Book | 7 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *The Right It* — Alberto Savoia | Book | 5 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| "Dual-Track Agile" — Marty Cagan | Article | 20 min | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| producttalk.org — Teresa Torres | Blog | Ongoing | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why Torres over Cagan here?** Cagan tells you *that* you should do discovery. Torres tells you *how* to do it weekly, with a real team. Start with Torres.

**Practice:** For 2 consecutive weeks, conduct at least 2 user interviews per week without pitching any solution. Map your findings on an opportunity solution tree. Identify the top 3 unmet needs.

---

### Block 2 · Jobs to Be Done

> **Goal:** Find the real job a user is hiring your product to do — so you build what they need, not what they asked for.
> **Key concepts:** Functional / emotional / social jobs, switch triggers, demand-side sales, the milkshake study.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Competing Against Luck* — Clayton Christensen | Book | 6 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *When Coffee and Kale Compete* — Alan Klement | Book | 5 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| Demand-Side Sales — Bob Moesta | Podcast episodes | 2 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| JTBD guide — Intercom | eBook | 1 hr | ⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *Competing Against Luck* over *When Coffee and Kale Compete*?** Christensen's framing is more broadly applicable; Klement's is deeper on consumer products. Read Christensen first; Klement is the follow-up.

**Practice:** Write a JTBD statement for your product's top 3 use cases: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]." Then identify what your users currently use as alternatives to hire your product.

---

### Block 3 · Prioritization

> **Goal:** Make defensible tradeoff decisions when everything feels urgent.
> **Key concepts:** RICE, ICE, opportunity scoring, GIST, cost of delay, when to say no and how.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Inspired* — Marty Cagan (roadmap chapters) | Book | 2 hrs (partial) | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *The Principles of Product Development Flow* — Don Reinertsen | Book | 10 hrs | ⭐⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| GIST framework — Itamar Gilad | Articles | 1 hr | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| Reforge: Prioritization mental models | Articles | 2 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why not just use RICE?** RICE is a tool, not a framework. Reinertsen teaches you *why* certain things should be prioritized — the economics underneath. Read Reinertsen so RICE makes sense.

**Practice:** Take your current backlog (or any product's imagined backlog). Score every item with RICE. Then re-score using cost of delay. Present the difference and what it means for what you'd build next.

### Block 4 · Outcomes Over Output

> **Goal:** Shift from "did we ship?" to "did it work?" as your definition of done.
> **Key concepts:** Output vs outcome vs impact, escaping the build trap, outcome-based roadmaps, talking to leadership in outcomes.
> **Time:** 1 week

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Outcomes Over Output* — Josh Seiden | Book | 3 hrs | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Escaping the Build Trap* — Melissa Perri | Book | 5 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *The Goal* — Eliyahu Goldratt | Book | 8 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🇮🇷 |
| *User Story Mapping* — Jeff Patton | Book | 7 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *The Goal*?** It's fiction, not a PM book — but it rewires how you think about throughput vs activity. Read it once and you'll never confuse busy with productive again.

**Practice:** Rewrite your team's current roadmap in pure outcome language — no feature names, only metric changes and user behavior shifts. Then present it to one stakeholder and see if they understand it.

### Block 5 · Experimentation & A/B Testing

> **Goal:** Validate your bets before committing full engineering resources.
> **Key concepts:** Hypothesis structure, control vs treatment, statistical significance, when not to run an experiment, learning from negative results.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments* — Kohavi, Tang & Xu | Book | 12 hrs | ⭐⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Running Lean* — Ash Maurya | Book | 6 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| Experimentation guide — Optimizely | Online Guide | 1 hr | ⭐ | 🔴 |
| Ronny Kohavi's blog | Blog | Ongoing | ⭐⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why Kohavi?** It's dense but the only rigorous treatment of A/B testing at scale. Read the Optimizely guide first to build intuition, then use Kohavi as a reference when running real experiments.

**Practice:** Design a full A/B test plan for one specific product hypothesis: write the hypothesis, define the control and treatment, choose a primary metric and guardrail metrics, calculate the required sample size, and define what "learning" looks like even if the test fails.

### Block 7 · Influence Without Authority

> **Goal:** Lead engineers, designers, and stakeholders who don't report to you.
> **Key concepts:** Building trust, framing problems not solutions, negotiation, managing disagreement, earning credibility through competence.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks (and ongoing)

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Influence Without Authority* — Cohen & Bradford | Book | 6 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

### Ready for Senior PM when:
- Handles ambiguous problems without needing their manager to frame it first
- Shapes roadmap direction for their area — not just grooms the backlog
- Is sought out by engineering leads and designers for judgment on hard calls
- Can articulate a clear strategy for their domain: what they're building, why, and what they're *not* building
- Has run discovery and shipped something that moved a meaningful metric

## Level 3 — Senior Product Manager

**Time in role before moving up:** 24–36 months of influencing strategy across multiple teams

### Minimum viable path (if you only do 3 blocks)
1. Block 1 — Strategy Foundations *(without this, everything is tactics)*
2. Block 2 — Product Vision *(strategy needs a north star)*
3. Block 5 — Executive Storytelling *(strategy is worthless if you can't sell it)*

### Core outcomes (Cagan / *Empowered*)

| # | Outcome |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Product Vision** — crafts a compelling, inspiring vision for their domain that motivates the team |
| 2 | **Strategic Problem Framing** — identifies the *right* problems to solve; works with ambiguity at the business level |
| 3 | **Influence Across Teams** — shapes decisions and priorities across squads without direct authority |
| 4 | **Coaching** — raises the quality of discovery and delivery in teams around them; mentors junior PMs |
| 5 | **Executive Alignment** — earns trust of C-level and presents strategy with conviction |

---

### Block 1 · Strategy Foundations

> **Goal:** Tell the difference between a real strategy and a list of goals — and build real ones.
> **Key concepts:** Diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent actions (Rumelt); "where to play / how to win" (Martin); avoiding the "bad strategy" traps.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* — Richard Rumelt | Book | 8 hrs | ⭐⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Understanding Michael Porter* — Joan Magretta | Book | 6 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *The Art of War* — Sun Tzu | Book | 2 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🇮🇷 |
| SVPG articles on product strategy — Marty Cagan | Articles | 2 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why Rumelt first?** Most strategy frameworks teach you to fill in boxes. Rumelt teaches you what strategy *is*. After Rumelt, you'll recognize bad strategy everywhere — including your own.

**Practice:** Write a 1-page strategy document for your current product area using Rumelt's kernel: diagnosis (what's the key challenge), guiding policy (what's your approach), and coherent actions (what specifically will you do). Share it with a senior leader and get brutal feedback.

---

### Block 2 · Product Vision

> **Goal:** Craft a compelling product vision that motivates your team and guides hard decisions for years.
> **Key concepts:** Vision vs strategy vs roadmap, emotional resonance, future-back thinking, communicating vision at different altitudes.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Inspired* — Marty Cagan (vision chapters, re-read) | Book | 2 hrs (partial) | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Start With Why* — Simon Sinek | Book | 5 hrs | ⭐ | 🇮🇷 |
| *The Innovator's Solution* — Clayton Christensen | Book | 8 hrs | ⭐⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| "Product Vision vs Strategy" — Marty Cagan | Article | 15 min | ⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *Start With Why* for a PM?** Because teams follow vision, not roadmaps. Sinek's framework helps you write vision that people actually remember and repeat.

**Practice:** Write a product vision for your area — 2–3 sentences, no jargon, that a new engineer could read and immediately know what you're building and why it matters. Test it: read it to 3 people and ask what they think you're building.

---

### Block 3 · Systems Thinking

> **Goal:** See the non-obvious leverage points and feedback loops in your product and market.
> **Key concepts:** Stocks and flows, reinforcing vs balancing loops, unintended consequences, system archetypes, limits to growth.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |
|----------|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| *Thinking in Systems* — Donella Meadows | Book | 6 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *The Fifth Discipline* — Peter Senge | Book | 10 hrs | ⭐⭐⭐ | 🔴 |
| *Thinking, Fast and Slow* — Daniel Kahneman | Book | 12 hrs | ⭐⭐⭐ | 🇮🇷 |
| *The Systems Bible* — John Gall | Book | 4 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *Thinking in Systems* before Kahneman?** Meadows gives you the vocabulary. Kahneman gives you the psychology. Build the structural model first, then add human behavior on top.

**Practice:** Draw a systems diagram for your product's main growth loop: what drives acquisition, engagement, and retention, and where do the feedback loops connect? Identify one leverage point you're currently ignoring.

---

### Block 4 · Platform & Ecosystem Thinking

> **Goal:** Design and evaluate products that create network effects and multi-sided value.
> **Key concepts:** Platform vs pipeline, network effects, cold start problem, platform governance, ecosystem incentives.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks

### Block 1 · Competitive & Market Strategy

> **Goal:** Identify the structural forces that determine whether your product wins or loses over the long run.
> **Key concepts:** Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers (scale economies, network effects, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power), sustainable competitive advantage.
> **Time:** 2–3 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |

**Practice:** Map your company's current competitive position against Helmer's 7 powers. Which do you have? Which is your strongest? Which could you build in the next 3 years? Write a 2-page power analysis and use it to guide your next major product bet.

### Block 4 · Product Culture

> **Goal:** Build a company culture where great product thinking is the norm, not the exception.
> **Key concepts:** Psychological safety, outcome-orientation, tolerance for failure, what Netflix / Spotify got right, how culture breaks under pressure.
> **Time:** 1–2 weeks

| Resource | Type | Read time | Difficulty | Persian |

| *No Rules Rules* — Reed Hastings & Erin Meyer | Book | 7 hrs | ⭐⭐ | 🔴 |

> **Why *The Culture Code* over *No Rules Rules* first?** Coyle shows you the building blocks of culture at the scientific level — safety, sharing vulnerability, establishing purpose. Then Netflix shows you one radical implementation. Understand the principles before the case study.

**Practice:** Write your product culture manifesto — 5 specific, observable behaviors you want to see in your org, with one example of each and one anti-pattern to watch for. Share it publicly inside the company. Then audit how well your own behavior models each one.
